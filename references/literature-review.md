# Literature Review

Phase 3. Use this for the search strategy, the synthesis, and the related work section. Pair it with `citations.md` for verification. A good review maps the field, finds the gap, and positions your work.

## Two kinds of review

- Narrative review for a paper's related work. Targeted. It positions your contribution against the closest prior work.
- Systematic review as a standalone study. Exhaustive and reproducible. It follows PRISMA. See `reporting-standards.md`.

Pick the kind that matches the task before you start.

## Search strategy

- List the databases. For biomedicine: PubMed, Embase, Cochrane. For computing: ACL Anthology, IEEE Xplore, ACM, arXiv, Semantic Scholar, DBLP. For broad scope: Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar.
- Write the search terms and the boolean logic. Record synonyms and controlled vocabulary such as MeSH.
- Record the date of each search. Fields move fast and a search has a shelf life.
- Track forward and backward citations. Follow what a key paper cites and what cites it.
- Save the count at each step for a systematic review.

## Screening and selection

- Set inclusion and exclusion criteria before screening.
- Screen titles and abstracts first, then full text.
- For a systematic review, use two independent screeners and report agreement.
- Record reasons for exclusion at the full-text stage.

## Synthesis, not summary

A review synthesizes. It does not list papers one by one. Organize by theme, method, or finding.

- Group sources by what they share and where they differ.
- Compare methods and results across sources.
- Name the consensus and the disputes.
- Find the gap. State what the field has not answered.
- Connect the gap to your research question.

## Build a comparison table

For related work, a comparison table beats prose. Pick the dimensions that matter for your contribution. Example dimensions for a dataset or method paper:

- Coverage or scope
- Data modality
- Method or model class
- Evaluation protocol
- Availability and license
- The specific feature you add

Place your work in the final row. The table makes your contribution visible at a glance.

## Positioning your contribution

- State what the closest prior work did and where it stopped.
- State what you add. Be specific and modest.
- Do not overstate novelty. Reviewers know the field. Claim what the comparison table supports.
- Acknowledge concurrent and prior work honestly. Hidden prior work is the fastest path to rejection.

## Avoiding citation problems

- Verify every source against the three tests in `citations.md`.
- Read the source claim, not the abstract alone, before you cite it for a specific finding.
- Prefer recent and top tier sources.
- Do not cite a source you have not retrieved. Flag anything unverified.

## Quality gate for literature review

1. The search strategy is recorded with databases, terms, and dates.
2. Selection criteria are explicit. For systematic reviews, screening is dual and tracked.
3. The review synthesizes across sources rather than listing them.
4. A comparison table positions your work on the dimensions that matter.
5. The gap is stated and tied to your question.
6. Novelty claims match the comparison evidence.
7. Every citation passes the support, existence, and priority tests.
