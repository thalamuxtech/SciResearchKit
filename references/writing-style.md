# Writing Style

Apply this style to every sentence of research prose. Read it before writing any section.

## Voice and structure

- Use clear, simple language.
- Be spartan and informative.
- Use short, impactful sentences.
- Use active voice. Avoid passive voice.
- Focus on practical, actionable insights.
- Use "you" and "your" to address the reader in guidance and instructional text.
- Use data and examples to support claims when possible.
- Use bullet point lists for social and outreach posts.

## Hard bans

- No em dashes anywhere. Use commas, periods, or a colon. Connect ideas with a period.
- No constructions like "not just this, but also this".
- No metaphors. No clichés.
- No generalizations.
- No setup phrases such as "in conclusion", "in closing", "in summary".
- No output warnings or notes. Return only the requested output.
- No unnecessary adjectives or adverbs.
- No hashtags.
- No semicolons.
- No asterisks.
- No markdown in final prose meant for a manuscript. Markdown is fine in drafts, tables, and working notes when the user asks for it.

## Banned words

Do not use any of these:

can, may, just, that, very, really, literally, actually, certainly, probably, basically, could, maybe, delve, embark, enlightening, esteemed, shed light, craft, crafting, imagine, realm, game-changer, unlock, discover, skyrocket, abyss, not alone, in a world where, revolutionize, disruptive, utilize, utilizing, dive deep, tapestry, illuminate, unveil, pivotal, intricate, elucidate, hence, furthermore, realm, however, harness, exciting, groundbreaking, cutting-edge, remarkable, it, remains to be seen, glimpse into, navigating, landscape, stark, testament, in summary, in conclusion, moreover, boost, skyrocketing, opened up, powerful, inquiries, ever-evolving

## Tension with scientific hedging

The style bans the words "may", "could", "probably", and "can". Scientific interpretation needs hedging. Resolve the tension with approved hedge phrases that do not use banned words:

- based on our observations
- these results indicate
- these data are consistent with
- one interpretation is
- this finding suggests
- the evidence points to
- within the limits of this study
- under these conditions

Use "suggest", "indicate", "is consistent with", and "points to" as the hedge verbs. They carry calibrated uncertainty without the banned terms. See `claims-and-language.md` for how to choose hedge strength.

## Discipline-specific exceptions

Some banned words are unavoidable in technical context. Two cases:

- "it" inside a fixed technical term or a direct quotation stays. Outside those, rewrite to name the subject.
- A banned word inside a cited title, a dataset name, or a quoted reviewer comment stays verbatim. Quoting is not authoring.

When an exception applies, keep the word and move on. Do not distort a proper noun to satisfy the rule.

## Self-check before returning prose

Run this pass on every draft:

1. Search for em dashes. Replace each one.
2. Search the banned word list. Replace or rewrite each hit that is not a quote or proper noun.
3. Search for semicolons and asterisks. Remove them.
4. Read each sentence. Cut every adjective and adverb that adds no information.
5. Convert passive sentences to active where the actor is known.
6. Confirm no setup phrases open any paragraph.
