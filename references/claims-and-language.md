# Claims and Language

Every claim must match its evidence. Overconfident claims are the most common reason reviewers reject results and discussion sections. This file sets the rule for matching language to evidence strength.

## The core principle

Write the weakest claim the data support, not the strongest claim the data allow. Reviewers trust calibrated authors. They distrust authors who oversell.

## Evidence-to-language ladder

Match the verb to the design and the statistics.

| Evidence strength | Design example | Approved language |
|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Causal, high power, preregistered | RCT, large n, replicated | these results indicate, the data show |
| Strong association, controlled | Cohort, adjusted, large n | these data are consistent with, the evidence points to |
| Moderate association | Cross-sectional, observational | this finding suggests, one interpretation is |
| Weak or exploratory | Small n, post hoc, pilot | based on our observations, these data raise the possibility |
| Descriptive only | Case series, qualitative | we observed, we report |

Never climb above the row your design sits in. A cross-sectional study does not show causation. A pilot does not establish effect size.

## Words to avoid in results and discussion

These verbs claim more than most studies support. Replace them.

- prove, proves, proven. Replace with: the data are consistent with.
- confirm, confirms. Replace with: these results support.
- establish, establishes. Replace with: these results indicate, within the limits of this study.
- demonstrate causality from observational data. Replace with: this finding suggests an association.
- significant used to mean important. Reserve "significant" for statistical significance with a stated test and threshold.

## Hedging without banned words

The writing style bans "may", "could", "probably". Use these instead:

- this finding suggests
- one interpretation is
- these data are consistent with
- the evidence points to
- within the limits of this study
- under these conditions
- based on our observations

## Required qualifiers

Attach the relevant qualifier to interpretive claims:

- Sample and scope. State the population the claim covers. Do not generalize beyond the sample.
- Uncertainty. Report the confidence interval or the credible interval, not the point estimate alone.
- Design limits. Name the design constraint that bounds the claim, such as confounding, selection, or measurement error.
- Replication status. State whether the finding is new, replicated, or contradicts prior work.

## Statistical language discipline

- A p value above your threshold means the test did not detect an effect. It does not mean no effect exists. Write "we did not detect a difference", not "there is no difference".
- Report effect sizes with intervals. A p value alone is not a result.
- Distinguish pre-specified from exploratory analyses. Label exploratory findings as exploratory.
- Do not interpret a non-significant result as evidence of equivalence unless you ran an equivalence test.
- Multiple comparisons inflate false positives. State the correction used.

## Results versus discussion

- Results state what you found. No interpretation. No mechanism.
- Discussion interprets. Each interpretive sentence carries a hedge from the approved list and a qualifier.
- Limitations come before the conclusion paragraph.

## Quality gate for claims

Before returning any results or discussion text:

1. For each claim, find the supporting number, figure, or citation. If none exists, cut the claim or hedge it.
2. Check each verb against the evidence ladder. Lower any verb that climbs above the design.
3. Confirm every interpretive sentence has a hedge and a scope qualifier.
4. Confirm "significant" appears only with a test and threshold.
5. Confirm no claim generalizes past the sample.
